## Notes on deviations from spec

### `inter_korean_summit` — 451 rather than 455 rows

The article-level source has 455 rows. After cleaning, 4 rows were dropped
because their `text` field was empty or fewer than 100 characters (likely
download failures in the upstream scraper). The remainder of the source
shape matches the spec exactly (article-level, not sentence-level).

### `colonial_magazines` — 495 rather than 500 rows

Stratified sampling across 19 magazine titles with a baseline of 18
articles per magazine plus a proportional bonus for the four big titles
landed at 495 after rounding. Could be bumped to exactly 500 by tweaking
the bonus distribution, but 495 is well within the spirit of the spec.

The spec listed five source files (kaebyok.csv, samcheolli.csv,
byeolgeongon.csv, donggwang.csv, other_magazines.csv) — all five exist
and combine to ~15,326 articles in the source. `other_magazines.csv`
itself contains 15 distinct minor titles, so the sample touches **19
magazines total** (4 big + 15 small).

### `korean_newspaper_archive_modern` — 2,000 rather than 2,500 rows; truncate to 2,000 chars rather than 3,500

After applying the spec filters (`year >= 1920` AND `language ==
'Modern Korean'` AND `text length > 500`), only 2,823 candidate articles
remain. The bulk are short 1940s-1950s regional press articles, but
even the slim CSV at 2,500 articles × 3,500-char truncation came in
at 5.92 MB — over the 5 MB cap.

To land safely under the cap, I:
- Tightened the per-document truncation from 3,500 to 2,000 characters
  (median article in the candidate pool is ~720 chars, so very few
  articles are actually truncated).
- Dropped the row count from 2,500 to 2,000 articles.

Result: 2,000 articles, 4.51 MB. Both `language` and `script` columns
exist in every source file; the build uses `language` for filtering as
specified. The 14 distinct newspapers in the sample are dominated by
**남조선민보** (~80% of the pool — late 1940s liberation-era Busan
regional daily) and **마산일보** (~37% — 1950s Korean War-era Masan
regional daily). This skew is a faithful artefact of which long-form
Modern-Korean newspaper articles survive in the NIKH archive.

### `nkmigrants_interviews` — 6,023 rather than 6,027 rows

4 rows dropped during cleaning for empty `response_text`. Negligible.

### `immigrant_interviews` — 1,006 rather than 1,008 rows

2 rows dropped during cleaning for empty `text`. Negligible.

### `kpoem` — emotion-aggregation choice

The source has five separate annotator columns (`annotator_01` ...
`annotator_05`), each containing a comma-separated list of emotion
labels. I aggregated by **pooling all labels across all five annotators
and selecting the most-voted label** as `dominant_emotion`. I also
expose `emotion_votes` (count of mentions for the dominant label) as a
confidence proxy. This is documented in both `README.md` and
`data_dictionary.md`.

The five annotators were not retained in the slim CSV — students who
want the raw label vectors can re-derive from the source TSV. The
dominant-label aggregation is enough for sentiment / clustering
research questions at the BA2 level.

## Suggestions for menu-level README (out of scope here, just notes)

1. **The two short-text corpora (immigrant_interviews + nkmigrants_interviews)
   should carry an explicit warning** in the menu description that LDA
   topic modelling will not work at the response level. Both CSVs are
   already README-flagged, but the menu README should reinforce this so
   students don't pick them and then get confused when LDA produces
   noise.
2. **The English-language Rodong corpus needs a method-compatibility
   warning** at the menu level: KNU + KLUE BERT will not work; students
   need to use VADER / a custom dictionary / English BERT instead.
3. **The colonial_magazines corpus uses Hanmun-mixed Korean** and
   pre-modern orthography in places. Modern KNU and KLUE BERT will be
   noisy on this register. The README flags this as a methodological
   reflection point — but worth surfacing at the menu level so students
   pick it as a deliberate choice, not by accident.
4. **Sample-balance varies across datasets**:
   - Perfectly balanced: `authoritarian_speeches` (200 per president).
   - Roughly balanced: `colonial_magazines` (18-83 per magazine), `kpoem`
     (615 — no group dimension), `kaebyok` (38-63 per year except the
     1934-35 tail).
   - Imbalanced (faithful to source): `inter_korean_summit` (heavily
     2018), `kr_newspapers_twitter` (left-leaning slightly larger than
     right), `korean_newspaper_archive_modern` (heavily 1940s-1950s
     and one-newspaper-dominated), `rodong_sinmun_en` (year-proportional
     to source).

## Reproducibility

Each dataset's `build.py` reads from
`/PATH/TO/scdenney/nlp_corpora/data/<source>/`
and writes the slim CSV next to the script. To regenerate any single
dataset:

```bash
cd private/week12/datasets/<short_name>
python3 build.py
```

To regenerate all 10 (from this directory):

```bash
for d in authoritarian_speeches inter_korean_summit colonial_magazines \
         kaebyok kr_newspapers_twitter kpoem immigrant_interviews \
         nkmigrants_interviews korean_newspaper_archive_modern \
         rodong_sinmun_en; do
  echo ">>> $d <<<"
  python3 "$d/build.py"
done
```

`random_state=42` everywhere — outputs are deterministic.
